## 1 · Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, students should be able to:

1. **Compare** the biological inefficiency of human learning with the statistical efficiency of AI learning.
2. **Understand** Charles Pépin's concept of humans as "denatured" or "failed" animals whose weakness generates freedom.
3. **Apply** the philosophical frameworks of George Berkeley and Maurice Merleau-Ponty to AI perception.
4. **Critically evaluate** whether AI systems truly *perceive* the world or merely *compute* data.

## 2 · Key Concepts & Definitions

### 2.1 The Failed Animal (Charles Pépin)

Humans differ from other animals through a profound **lack of instinct**. 
- **The Foal vs. The Baby:** A foal can walk within minutes of birth, guided by strong instincts. A human baby fails thousands of times over months before walking.
- **The Concept:** Humans are biologically "weak" and inefficient. However, this deficiency forces us to learn through trial, error, and perseverance. 
- **The AI Contrast:** AI is designed for speed, optimization, and minimizing failure. Humans learn *because* they fail; AI learns *to avoid* failure.

### 2.2 Tâtonnement (Trial and Error)

The human process of stumbling, guessing, and correcting. Because humans lack instinct, they must improvise. This inefficiency builds adaptability and creates meaning out of error, whereas AI statistically optimizes to eliminate error.

### 2.3 Compensation and Culture

Because of our biological weakness, humans must compensate through **social learning**—culture, education, empathy, and relationships. AI can absorb informational data instantly, but it lacks the relational, emotional context that drives human compensation.

### 2.4 Existence Precedes Essence (Sartre) / Freedom

Because humans are "failed animals" not bound by strict programming or instinct, we are not predetermined. This indeterminacy is the root of human **freedom and invention**. AI, with its fixed goals and mathematical optimization, does not experience this existential freedom.

## 3 · Key Philosophical Lenses on Perception

### 3.1 George Berkeley (Idealism)

**Core Idea:** *"To be is to be perceived" (esse est percipi).* Objects exist only insofar as they are perceived by a mind.

**Application to AI:** 
- If an AI system's sensors are suddenly disconnected, does its "world" still exist? 
- In virtual reality or simulation environments, AI operates in a world that only exists when computed. Does AI "create" or "discover" its world?
- Berkeley’s framework challenges us to ask if data storage counts as ongoing perception.

### 3.2 Maurice Merleau-Ponty (Phenomenology of Embodiment)

**Core Idea:** The perceiver and the perceived world cannot be disentangled. Perception is fundamentally **embodied**—rooted in physical, lived, situated experience.

**Application to AI:**
- A robot's physical form (sensors, shape, movement) completely shapes what it can perceive. 
- Disembodied AI (like LLMs in a server) may process descriptions of the world, but without a body, it cannot achieve phenomenological, situated perception. 
- True perception happens *between* perceivers through interaction (e.g., human-robot social loops).

## 4 · Synthesis: Human vs. AI

### Human Learning vs. AI Learning

| Aspect | Human Learning | AI Learning |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin** | Weak instincts → learning through trial and error | Strong optimization → learning through data efficiency |
| **Method** | Experiential, social, embodied | Computational, statistical |
| **Relation to Error**| Failure is essential and meaningful | Error is minimized and avoided |
| **Outcome** | Freedom and self-invention | Performance and prediction |

### Human Perception vs. AI Perception

| Aspect | Human Perception | AI Perception |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Embodied, sensory, subjective | Data-driven, computational |
| **Relation to World**| Interdependent — shaped by action | One-directional — world as data input |
| **Continuity** | Continuous and experiential | Discrete and episodic |
| **Awareness** | Conscious and interpretive | Non-conscious and procedural |

## 5 · In-Class Activities

Through the session, practical and physical exercises were used to translate these abstract philosophies into tangible design questions:

1. **The Foal vs. the Baby:** Comparing instinctive success (foal) to learning through failure (human). 
2. **Virtues in Failure:** Brainstorming what knowledge arises *only* through failure—and what AI misses by not feeling the "cost" of failure.
3. **The Compensation Audit:** Classifying human learning into "Data" vs "Relational" and questioning if AI can ever replace the social category.
4. **Data vs. Experience:** Sorting tasks (e.g., "detecting sadness in a voice" vs "feeling sad because of the tone") to draw the line between AI processing and human experiencing.
5. **If the Sensors Go Dark:** Applying Berkeley to an AI vision system turned off. Does its world still exist in the parameters?
6. **Embodied or Disembodied? (Merleau-Ponty):** Blind navigation exercises and touch recognition to prove how much knowledge the *body* provides, emphasizing why a body-less AI lacks true perception.
7. **AI as a Philosopher:** Prompting an LLM to act as Berkeley or Merleau-Ponty. Exploring whether the LLM *understands* the philosophy or just statistically *recombines* it.

## 6 · Assignment 1 Connections

Themes from this week provide powerful conceptual backing for **Assignment 1 (Digital Projects + Report)**:

- **The Value of Failure (Process over Polish):** If human creativity comes from inefficiency and error (Pépin), your report could reflect on how using highly-optimized AI tools removed the "productive struggle" from your art project. Did AI make the process too smooth? Did you lose creative freedom as a result?
- **Disembodied Art:** If you are creating visual or audio art, consider Merleau-Ponty. You (the human) perceive the world through your body; the AI generator produced the output purely through data. How does this difference manifest in the final artwork? Is it lacking "lived" texture?
- **Idealist Reality (Berkeley):** If your project involves generative video or 3D, consider Berkeley's *esse est percipi*. You are generating a world that only exists inside the AI's parameter space and the screen's pixels.

## 8 · Reflection Prompts

Use these prompts for your journal or to brainstorm your project report:

1. **The Cost of Optimization:** Think of a skill you learned where making mistakes was crucial to your understanding. What would happen if an AI tool prevented you from making those mistakes?
2. **Artificial "Perception":** When an LLM tells you it is "happy to help," it is simulating emotion based on data. How does Berkeley's view change how you interact with this illusion?
3. **Embodied Intelligence:** Try to explain the exact sensation of walking on gravel to a chat AI. Notice the impossible gap between your embodied experience (Merleau-Ponty) and the AI's linguistic approximation. What does this mean for AI-generated art?
