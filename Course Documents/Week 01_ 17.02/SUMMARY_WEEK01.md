## üí° Philosophical Arguments & Positions

### 1. Why Philosophy Belongs in an AI/ML Degree

- **Thesis:** AI development raises profound philosophical questions that technical training alone cannot address.
- **Reasoning:** As Demis Hassabis (co-founder of Google DeepMind, Nobel Prize winner) stated: *"I want to understand the big questions‚Ä¶ I thought building AI would be the fastest route to answer some of those questions."* Philosophy provides tools to grapple with questions about consciousness, creativity, ethics, and human identity ‚Äî all of which are at stake in AI development.
- **Counter-argument:** Some argue that philosophy is impractical for engineers ‚Äî code solves problems, not abstract debate.
- **Application to AI:** Understanding *what* intelligence, creativity, or art *is* determines how we design and evaluate AI systems. Without philosophical clarity, we risk building tools we cannot meaningfully assess.

### 2. Can AI Create Art?

- **Thesis (Pro):** AI can produce outputs that are visually, musically, or emotionally indistinguishable from human-created art. If the result moves people, it is art.
- **Reasoning:** The Gallery Walk demonstrated that audiences respond emotionally to works without knowing whether a human or machine created them. If emotional impact defines art, AI qualifies.
- **Counter-argument:** Art requires *intention* and *meaning*. AI has no consciousness, no life experience, and no purpose ‚Äî it produces patterns, not meaning. Its outputs are "pattern remixing," not creation.
- **Application to AI:** This debate directly frames Assignment 1 ‚Äî students must create AI art and reflect on whether their collaboration with AI constitutes genuine artistic creation.

### 3. Process vs. Result in Creativity

- **Thesis:** The creative *process* (struggle, intention, decision-making, revision) is what gives art its value ‚Äî not the final product alone.
- **Reasoning:** A painting by a human who spent months refining their vision carries a different kind of value than an identical image generated in seconds by Midjourney. The process encodes human experience.
- **Counter-argument:** Audiences rarely know or care about the process. A beautiful photograph is beautiful regardless of whether the photographer spent hours setting up the shot or got lucky. Similarly, an AI image that moves people has achieved art's purpose.
- **Application to AI:** This tension runs through the entire PHKI module. Assignment 1 explicitly asks students to reflect on the creative process ‚Äî not just submit a product.

### 4. The Three Lenses for the Future of Art

The slides introduce a structured framework for analysing AI art:

#### Philosophical Lens
- **Question:** If art is about intention and meaning, does AI art qualify? Or is it "pattern remixing"?
- **Implication:** Forces us to interrogate our *definition* of art before evaluating AI's role in it.

#### Economic Lens
- **Question:** Who gets paid, credited, or protected? The prompter? The AI company? The training-data artists?
- **Implication:** AI art disrupts traditional economic models. Artists whose work trained models receive no compensation; prompters claim authorship for minimal effort; AI companies profit from the entire ecosystem.

#### Institutional Lens
- **Question:** Will museums, galleries, and competitions accept AI art?
- **Implication:** Institutional gatekeeping shapes what is culturally recognised as "art." The case of Jason Allen winning the Colorado State Fair with *Th√©√¢tre D'op√©ra Spatial* (Midjourney) exemplifies this tension.

## üó£Ô∏è Discussion Building Blocks

### Pro-Arguments (AI can be an artist)

- "If the purpose of art is to evoke emotion and provoke thought, then AI art succeeds ‚Äî audiences respond to it without knowing or caring about its origin."
- "Art has always been mediated by technology ‚Äî from the printing press to photography to digital editing. AI is simply the next tool in this continuum."
- "The prompter's creative vision, combined with iterative refinement, constitutes a genuine creative process ‚Äî just as a photographer *chooses* rather than *paints*."

### Counter-Arguments (AI cannot be an artist)

- "Art requires conscious intention and lived experience. An AI has neither ‚Äî its outputs are statistical artefacts, not expressions of meaning."
- "If everyone can generate images with a text prompt, art loses the scarcity and skill that give it cultural value."
- "AI art is built on the unpaid labour of human artists whose works were scraped into training datasets ‚Äî this is exploitation, not creation."

### Bridging Statements

- "Perhaps the question is not whether AI *is* an artist, but whether the *collaboration* between human and AI constitutes a new form of creative practice."
- "We can value AI art aesthetically while still recognising that it raises serious ethical and economic concerns for human artists."
- "The debate may ultimately hinge on what we *want* art to be ‚Äî a mirror of human experience, or a source of beauty regardless of origin."

### Critical Questions

- Who benefits when we call AI outputs "art" ‚Äî the prompter, the AI company, or society?
- If you cannot tell whether a painting was made by a human or AI, does the answer to that question change its value? Why?
- Is the discomfort many feel about AI art rooted in a genuine philosophical objection, or in a fear of economic displacement?
- Should artists have the right to opt out of training datasets ‚Äî and what would the consequences be?

## üìã In-Class Activities Summary

### Activity 1: Gallery Walk ‚Äî "What do you value in different art mediums?"

- **Description:** Students viewed 10 works across different media (film, painting, architecture, performance art, video games, music, drones, photography, poetry). For each, they answered: *(1) What are your impressions? How does it make you feel? (2) What stands out?*
- **Purpose:** To surface personal aesthetic values before any philosophical framework is applied ‚Äî creating an honest baseline of what students care about in art.
- **Discussion Prompts (in pairs/small groups):**
  - How do you differ from your partner in what you value?
  - Can AI create, tap into, or evoke these qualities as well?
  - Would you care if/that they were AI-created?
- **Key Takeaway:** Different people value different things in art (skill, emotion, novelty, beauty, message). Understanding your own values is essential before evaluating whether AI can deliver them.

### Activity 2: Opinion Spectrum ‚Äî AI Art Statements

- **Description:** Students positioned themselves on a "Strongly Disagree ‚Üî Strongly Agree" spectrum for 8 provocative statements:
  1. "AI art should be displayed alongside traditional art in museums."
  2. "I would pay the same for AI art as for human-created art."
  3. "It matters to me whether art was created by a human."
  4. "AI art can evoke genuine human emotions."
  5. "If everyone can generate art with AI, then art loses its value."
  6. "AI art challenges what it means to be creative."
  7. "Artists should have the right to opt-out of training datasets."
  8. "The future of art will be AI-human collaboration, not replacement."
- **Purpose:** To make personal positions visible and debatable ‚Äî a starting point for developing nuanced philosophical arguments over the semester.
- **Key Takeaway:** Most positions are defensible; the value lies in *why* you hold a position, not just *that* you hold it.

### Activity 3: Resource Preparation for Week 2

- **Task:** Pick one of six provided resources (papers, articles, podcasts, videos) and prepare to discuss: *(1) What is the main argument? (2) What future does it imagine? (3) Do you agree?*
- **Tip from the slides:** "Take note of each publisher/company. Is it possible that they are trying to push a certain narrative? Who is funding them?" ‚Äî introduction to critical source evaluation.
