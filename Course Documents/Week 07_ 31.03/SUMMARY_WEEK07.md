## 2 · Key Concepts & Comparisons

### 2.1 The Common Ground: Trial and Error

Learning through trial and error is a fundamental process shared by humans and AI. For humans, it involves making mistakes, experiencing the emotional weight of those errors (regret, frustration), and adjusting behavior to find meaning. 

For AI, "trial and error" refers to reinforcement learning and gradient descent: processing data, making predictions, receiving loss/feedback, and adjusting weights. 

**The divergence:** AI optimizes an external reward function without self-awareness; humans use metacognition to deeply reflect on *why* the error occurred.

### 2.2 The Six Mechanisms of "Learning to Learn"

The core of the lecture examined human cognitive mechanisms vs AI equivalents based on Grégoire Borst's *Votre cerveau* series.

#### 1. Attention
- **Human:** Selective focus on relevant stimuli, akin to a spotlight. It is limited, easily hijacked (attentional blindness, digital distractions), and fueled by curiosity.
- **AI:** Programmed attention mechanisms (like the Transformer's self-attention). Efficient, mathematical prioritization unaffected by boredom or distraction.

#### 2. Memory
- **Human:** Divided into working memory (limited capacity, expanded via chunking) and long-term memory. Requires deep semantic processing and associative linking to encode effectively.
- **AI:** Massive, hyper-efficient data storage. However, it lacks genuine associative meaning; retrieval is based on mathematical proximity rather than lived, emotional context.

#### 3. Inhibition and Flexibility
- **Human:** The prefrontal cortex enables us to override automatic, intuitive responses (e.g., Stroop Effect) to apply logical reasoning. Inhibition supports creativity and empathy.
- **AI:** Does not process "habit" versus "logic" in the same way. It predicts the most statistically likely response unless artificially constrained via explicit instructions or guardrails.

#### 4. Metacognition
- **Human:** The ability to evaluate and monitor one's own learning ("thinking about thinking"). Essential for recognizing weaknesses, adjusting strategies, and achieving autonomy.
- **AI:** Currently lacks genuine metacognition or self-awareness. It can be prompted to "reflect" or verify its own steps (Chain-of-Thought), but this is simulated output rather than conscious self-evaluation.

#### 5. Motivation & Spirit for Change
- **Human:** Driven by both extrinsic (rewards) and intrinsic (curiosity, growth mindset, satisfaction) motivation.
- **AI:** Programmed solely around extrinsic optimization (loss functions, reward models in RLHF). AI experiences no intrinsic joy in discovery.

#### 6. Emotions!
- **Human:** Emotions are the engines of learning. Complex emotions like regret and relief guide decision-making, highlight what matters, and solidify memory.
- **AI:** Emotionless. It can analyze the semantic markers of human emotion in text, but possesses no internal state.

## 3 · Key Arguments & Positions

### 3.1 Cory Doctorow: "LLMs are slot-machines"

An argument discussed in relation to "ChatGPT in shopping" and monetization. 
- **The Argument:** When users pay by interaction (the "pull of the handle"), the vendor's commercial incentive is *not* to solve the problem immediately in one prompt. Instead, the system is incentivized to give the *appearance* of progress, keeping the user engaged and querying longer.
- **Why it matters:** It forces us to ask whether AI tools are optimized for our learning/efficiency, or optimized for user retention and monetization.

## 4 · Synthesis: The Self-Regulating Learner

| Function | Human Cognitive Role | Educational / AI Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Attention** | Focus on relevance | Humans must manage digital overload; AI just computes vectors. |
| **Memory** | Store and organize information | Humans build expertise; AI provides instant retrieval without understanding. |
| **Inhibition** | Resist automatism, adapt | Humans must suppress biases for logic; AI must have guardrails tuned. |
| **Metacognition** | Reflect on learning processes | Self-regulated learning; AI cannot genuinely self-evaluate. |
| **Motivation** | Drive engagement | Intrinsic growth; AI only follows extrinsic programmed bounds. |
| **Emotions** | Energize and regulate | Meaning-making; AI is completely devoid of this dimension. |

## 8 · Reflection Prompts

1. **The Slot-Machine Interface:** Look at your prompt history with ChatGPT or Midjourney. Do you ever fall into a loop of rapid, unreflective prompting? How does this affect your learning compared to slow, intentional problem-solving?
2. **Inhibition & AI:** We use inhibition to stop ourselves from blurting out the first intuitive (and often wrong) answer. Does AI have an "intuitive" response (the statistically most likely next word)? Does it have a mechanism to inhibit itself?
3. **Emotional Memory:** Think of an educational experience you remember vividly. Was it tied to a strong emotion (embarrassment, profound awe, connection)? If AI tutors cannot share or elicit genuine emotion, what is the ceiling on their effectiveness?
