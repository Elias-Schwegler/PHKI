# PHKI – Week 03: History of "Man vs. Machine"
**Date:** 03.03.2026 | **Lecturer:** Catherine Hayden (CH)

## 🎯 Learning Objectives
- **Contextualise** major historical moments of technological change, noting both societal hopes and anxieties.
- **Identify recurring patterns** in societal responses to technological shifts (e.g., Industrial Revolution, Cold War).
- **Understand** why historical analysis is essential for critically assessing emerging Artificial Intelligence systems.
- **Analyse** how political, economic, and cultural structures inherently shape technological outcomes.

## 💡 Philosophical Arguments & Positions

- **Argument: History provides a crucial framing for AI, despite its novel cognitive capabilities.**
  - **Thesis:** While AI seems entirely new, examining the Industrial Revolution and Cold War reveals consistent patterns of structural disruption, power concentration, and societal anxiety.
  - **Reasoning:** Technologies are always embedded in social structures. Who benefits (CEOs, owners), who pays the cost (displaced workers), and whose labour is hidden remain consistent themes.
  - **Counter-arguments:** AI is immaterial (code vs. physical factories/uranium) and cognitive, leading to fundamentally different challenges that historical analogies (like nuclear treaties) cannot fully map onto.
  - **Application to AI:** Helps us anticipate wealth inequality and the need for robust worker protections and new regulatory bodies.

- **Argument: Geopolitical competition drives innovation at the expense of safety.**
  - **Thesis:** The fear of losing global dominance accelerates AI deployment beyond our capacity to govern it safely.
  - **Reasoning:** Corporate-led frontier research, fueled by nationalist rhetoric, prioritizes speed over establishing the necessary guardrails.
  - **Counter-arguments:** Competition has historically yielded immense benefits: medical breakthroughs, economic growth, and expanded access to knowledge.
  - **Application to AI:** Pushes the debate on international AI governance. Can we create an "IAEA for AI" without waiting for a crisis moment?

- **Argument: AI's "magic" is built on exploited, hidden human labour.**
  - **Thesis:** AI systems are deeply reliant on marginalized humans performing traumatic and underpaid tasks (e.g., data labeling, content moderation).
  - **Reasoning:** Case studies (Sama / OpenAI / Meta) show workers in Kenya and the Philippines paid $1.50–$3.74/hour to review graphic material, suffering psychological trauma.
  - **Counter-arguments:** Tech companies argue that global outsourcing creates jobs and allows for culturally informed, 24/7 content review.
  - **Application to AI:** Shifts the ethical focus from abstract "AI safety" to immediate human rights and supply-chain transparency.

## 🗣️ Discussion Building Blocks

- **Pro-arguments (for AI's historical parallels):** "Just as the Industrial Revolution displaced skilled artisans, AI is poised to disrupt entry-level white-collar jobs, proving that technological transitions always require significant societal adaptation over decades."
- **Counter-arguments (against the Cold War analogy):** "Unlike nuclear material, which is scarce, centralized, and observable by satellites, AI code can be copied infinitely and trained secretly, making Cold War-style governance treaties nearly impossible to enforce."
- **Bridging statements:** "While the technology itself—neural networks instead of steam engines—is unprecedented, the economic incentives driving power concentration and the exploitation of marginalized workers remain largely the same."
- **Critical questions:** 
  - "Does the invisibility of AI labour matter morally, or is it only the poor working conditions that make it unethical?"
  - "Are AI companies fully responsible for the working conditions of outsourced labourers, or do we, as users benefiting from the tool, share that burden?"
  - "Should AI regulation wait for a catastrophe (like nuclear treaties often did), or can we proactively govern it?"

## 📋 In-Class Activities Summary

- **A Technology Timeline Analysis:** Students chose key moments from the Industrial Revolution and mapped out the associated hopes, fears, and power dynamics. This was then compared to the current Generative AI era to identify which lessons apply and where the analogy breaks down (e.g., blue-collar vs. white-collar disruption).
- **Cold War Analogy Discussion:** Drawing from the Politico article, students debated whether geopolitical rivalry accelerates GenAI today and whether an "IAEA for AI" could exist given the structural differences between nuclear material (observable) and AI code (invisible). 
- **Hidden Labour Case Study Review:** An in-depth look at OpenAI and Meta's outsourcing of content moderation to Sama in Kenya. Students discussed the ethical implications of $1.50/hr wages and the psychological trauma resulting from reviewing explicit material to make AI "safe" for Western users.

## 🎯 Project Relevance

### Assignment 1 (Digital Projects, 40%)
- **Conceptual Connection:** When using an AI tool for your artwork, reflect on whose labour made that tool possible. Was there hidden, low-wage labour used to train the model you are using? 
- **Report framing:** Address the "AI Cold War" acceleration. Did corporate design choices shape your artistic output? Did the tool impose a particular value set or political perspective? Did using AI increase your creative speed, and if so, did faster mean *better*?
- **Actionable Insight:** Consider contrasting the polished, "magical" output of your AI art with the invisible human cost in your analytical report. Reflect on whether using AI for art is ethically different from using sweatshop-produced art materials.

### Assignment 2 (Group Videos + Micro-viva, 60%)
- **Media Portrayals:** Look for media that portrays AI either as a rapid "arms race" (focusing on corporate/national power) or media that intentionally hides (or exposes) the human labour behind the machine.
- **Micro-viva Arguments:** Use the historical comparisons (Industrial Revolution, Cold War) as structural frameworks for analyzing your chosen film or book. Does the media treat AI as a completely new phenomenon, or does it root it in an existing capitalist/geopolitical struggle?
